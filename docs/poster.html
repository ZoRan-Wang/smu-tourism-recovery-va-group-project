Singapore Tourism Recovery Through Time-Series Visual Analytics

One shared data backbone for exploration, recovery-pattern clustering, and source-market forecasting

Xi Zixun - Wang Zhuoran - Jin Qinhao

Core target
Country-level monthly visitor arrivals

Supporting context
Hotel occupancy, stay length, room revenue

Delivery
Quarto website + modular Shiny app

Final workflow
Explore -> Cluster -> Forecast

Motivation and Problem

Singapore tourism did not recover as one smooth curve after the pandemic. Source markets returned at different speeds, while hotel utilisation and length-of-stay dynamics responded with their own lag. Static summary charts are not enough for users who need to:

  • compare recovery paths across source markets
  • identify common trajectory patterns
  • assess near-term demand using time-aware forecasting

This project therefore reframes tourism recovery as a visual time-series decision workflow rather than a single aggregate KPI story.

Data Backbone and Method

The final prototype uses one coordinated data contract:

  • Shared backbone: monthly visitor arrivals by country
  • Context layer: hotel occupancy, average length of stay, number of hotels, and room revenue

This keeps all modules consistent while still allowing the app to explain whether changes in arrivals are reflected in broader tourism performance.

Analytical Logic

  1. Explore trend, shock, rebound, and seasonality.
  2. Cluster country trajectories with similar recovery shapes.
  3. Forecast selected source markets with a time-aware holdout split.

Why This Design Works

  • It preserves one common analytical target across modules.
  • It supports both explanation and short-term planning.
  • It connects demand signals to business-facing tourism indicators.

Shiny App Experience

The project is delivered as a website-plus-Shiny system rather than a static report.

  • The website documents the data contract, prototypes, and methods.
  • The Shiny app exposes the final module flow in one place.
  • Package audit and smoke tests support deployment reliability.

User Flow

1. Explorer
Read raw series and confirm metadata.

2. Clustering
See which country trajectories recover in similar ways.

3. Forecasting
Benchmark Seasonal Naive, ETS, and ARIMA on the same testing horizon.

Time Series Explorer

The explorer establishes the narrative baseline: a collapse in 2020, a staggered reopening period, and an uneven return of source-market demand. It also tests whether demand recovery aligns with hotel utilisation.

Key Findings

  • China’s contribution clearly shows the magnitude of the pandemic shock.
  • Recovery is real, but it is not symmetric with the pre-2020 level.
  • Hotel occupancy still moves positively with arrivals, even when recovery is phased.

China and total arrivals collapse together in 2020, then recover at different speeds.

Demand recovery is positively associated with hotel occupancy, supporting the business interpretation of the series.

Clustering Recovery Trajectories

Clustering moves the project from “what happened” to “which markets behave similarly.” In the final design, the clustering unit is a country trajectory, not an individual month, which keeps the method aligned with the shared time-series backbone.

Key Findings

  • The reduced-space scatter separates recovery shapes into three interpretable groups.
  • The timeline indicates that the system moved through persistent phases rather than random monthly noise.
  • The elbow diagnostic supports a compact solution that remains explainable inside the Shiny app.

Three clusters separate high-performance, transition, and shock-related recovery patterns.

The market-state chronology reveals when the system leaves the shock period and enters recovery.

The elbow view keeps the clustering choice transparent rather than arbitrary.

Forecasting Source-Market Demand

The forecasting module follows the Chapter 19 and 20 workflow from R for Visual Analytics: inspect the time path, confirm seasonality, build a time-aware split, and compare benchmark versus model-based forecasts.

Key Findings

  • The selected country series preserves strong seasonality even after the shock period.
  • Decomposition makes the structural break explicit, which justifies a forecasting workflow.
  • Seasonal Naive, ETS, and ARIMA are compared on the same holdout window, keeping performance interpretation transparent.

China arrivals provide a clear example of shock, reopening, and renewed seasonal peaks.

Trend, seasonal, and remainder components reveal the structural change before forecasting begins.

The holdout comparison benchmarks Seasonal Naive, ETS, and ARIMA on one common testing horizon.

Key Takeaways and Next Step

What This Project Shows

  • Tourism recovery is better understood as a time-series system than as a single headline total.
  • Country-level arrivals provide one shared backbone that keeps exploration, clustering, and forecasting consistent.
  • Hotel occupancy and stay indicators add business meaning by showing whether demand recovery translates into tourism performance.
  • The final app supports comparison, explanation, and short-term planning in one workflow without relying on legacy tree-model panels.

Future Work

  • extend forecasting to more source markets
  • deploy the Shiny app to a public host
  • turn cluster interpretation into reusable market personas for decision support
